    Introduction

    This is the stark reality. However, when sovereign states began to exercise absolute authority within their territories, states started to put an end to the free movement of people and began intensifying control over immigration. Thus, governments can decide on who to admit or who to refuse as they become increasingly protective of their borders. The fact is, migration has remained and will always remain a common feature of human activity. And yet, most unfortunately, migration has seldom been accepted nor taken as a global policy priority and debate for the world until the 20th century. Globally, there are few legal frameworks governing the movement of people. One exception is the 1951 Convention relating to refugees, which states the right of refugees to leave their own countries and to be protected in international law. Even this law, which deals only with refugees. has lots of flaws and risks becoming obsolete. The most recent progress in this regard was the Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration, which was adopted on 19th December 2018 but has not been formally ratified.
